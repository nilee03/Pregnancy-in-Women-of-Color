Maternity History

Maternal Health in Women of color, specifically black women have had poor outcomes such as disproportionate high rates of deaths related to pregancy or childbirth. Socialital and health system factors both play a major part in this as well. Black women are more likely to experience less quality care in the medical systems, along with racial discrimination throughout their lives.


Because of racism,along with sexism and other systemic issues, that contribute to income inequality, Black Women are usually paid 63 cent for every dollar paid to White women. The minimum wage for Black women is $37,227 per year, which is $21,698 less than white women. This means that these women have less money to support themselves along with their families, and are more than likely have to choose between between essential resources like child care, housing, heath care, and food.


Compared to white women, Black women are are more likely to be unisured, less likely to acces prenatal care, and face greater financial care barriers when they need it. Because of this, Black women experience higher exposure rates to preventable diseases and chronic health conditions such as diabetes, hypertension, and cardiovascular disease. ANd this means that when, or if a Black Woman decides to have a baby, she is at a much higher risk of these conditions as well as the baby.

Overall

Black women deserve to have safe and healthy pregnancies and childbirth. To meaningfully improve Black maternal health outcomes, we need systemic change that starts with the health care system, improves access to care and makes the places Black women live and work healthier, more fair and more responsive to their needs.
